For those of us living in or near downtown Detroit, development is a constant topic of conversation. Many of us scoff, belittle and engage in loud displays of dismay at an $875 K condo for sale, or at the prospect of rent doubling should we move a few blocks away.

So: this is not a place for hardcore, thoroughly researched articles. It is a place for things I have seen (or heard, or imagined…), and where I put my urbanist hat on. This is a map of me running errands. I went to Whole Foods, Michigan First Credit Union, Marcus Market and Cass Corridog. We hear a good deal around here about the high rate of occupancy and the scarcity of apartments. Nevermind that almost every building I know of has multiple vacancies, that’s what we are told. So forget vacant apartment units: what about vacant lots?

Along my walk, I recorded what appeared to be 16 vacant lots. These lots are actually all or part of 35 parcels, covering over nine-and-a-half acres, seven of which are in Cass Corridor. 24 of these parcels are located south of Martin Luther King Blvd. Obviously, this is not all, or even the majority of vacant land in the Corridor. It is, however, a lot of vacant land to walk past while running errands. It is a lot of vacant land to walk past every day while hearing about scarcity, development and stressing about rising rents as luxury developments spring up across Woodward in Brush Park.

Land is not scarce here. There is room enough for all of us. It’s time for what I would call “normal people development.” But what does normal look like?

As reported by Violet Ikonomova at the Detroit Metro Times , “affordable” housing is based on Area Median Income (AMI) for the bizarrely-arrived-at Detroit-Warren-Livonia Metropolitan Statistical Area. Ikonomova reports that by lumping these municipalities in with Detroit, the AMI is drastically skewed from $26,000 (in Detroit city proper) to $69,000. Units designated “affordable” have rent set for those making 80% of the AMI, or about $40,000 per year.

This method puts “affordable” housing out of reach for over 60% of Detroit households, according to the U.S. Census Bureau.

What about Cass Corridor, though? Isn’t it already rapidly gentrifying? Isn’t it a place where the income outpaces the rest of Detroit proper? It is in Midtown, after all, right?

Nope. Wrong. Cass Corridor and Brush Park make up the majority of the 48201 ZIP code, which is actually one of the poorest in the city. The median household income is $13,964. Hey, you say: there’s a lot of college students there! Couldn’t that account for the low income? Nope. Only 10% of householders are under the age of 24, and the highest earners in the neighborhood are those in between the ages of 25 to 44 years old (median income for this group is a whopping $25,720!).

Let’s look at race: Median income for white households is $30 K, while the median income for Black households (who make up over 65% of the total population of Cass Corridor) is $11 K. At least half of both demographics are vulnerable, but white households are $20 K less vulnerable than black households. The list of statistics goes on, and while Cass Corridor is a very poor neighborhood despite its proximity to Wayne State, downtown and the hospital complex, it becomes clear that Cass Corridor is also a diverse neighborhood, with families, single people, old people, a mix of races and ethnicities, single mothers, college students and so on. That diversity is in danger under the current affordable housing model.

The long and short is that almost every narrative that is a proponent of development in greater downtown Detroit not only ignores the existence of the people who make up the neighborhood, but thrives at their expense. The land is there. Let's do something about housing now.
